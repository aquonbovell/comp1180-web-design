CLIMATE CHANGE

What is Climate Change?

Climate change is the long-term changing of a location's temperature and usual weather patterns. Climate change can relate to a specific place or the entire world. Weather patterns may become less predictable as a result of climate change. Because projected temperature and rainfall levels can no longer be depended on, these unexpected weather patterns might make it challenging to sustain and develop crops in farming regions. Climate change has also been linked to more frequent and powerful hurricanes, floods, downpours, and winter storms.Climate change is the long-term changing of a location's temperature and usual weather patterns.

Why is Climate Change a Serious Problem?

People all across the world are seeing personally how climate change may devastate the earth. Average temperatures that are steadily rising feed increasingly catastrophic wildfires, storms, and other calamities that are becoming hard to ignore. And, even while the globe is in the grip of a devastating pandemic, experts are warning that climate change is still the greatest threat to human health in recorded history. Even as wildfires raged across the United States, Europe, and Siberia in August, World Health Organization Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus stated in a statement that "the hazards presented by climate change might surpass those caused by any one illness."

Who is Most responsible?

One of the biggest fights at the United Nations climate summit in Glasgow is whether — and how — the world’s wealthiest nations, which are disproportionately responsible for global warming to date, should compensate poorer nations for the damages caused by rising temperatures. Rich countries, including the United States, Canada, Japan and much of western Europe, account for just 12 percent of the global population today but are responsible for 50 percent of all the planet-warming greenhouse gases released from fossil fuels and industry over the past 170 years. Over that period, the Earth's temperature has risen by around 1.1 degrees Celsius (2 degrees Fahrenheit), generating more powerful and deadly heat waves, floods, droughts, and wildfires. Poorer, more vulnerable countries have requested richer countries to contribute more funds to assist them adapt to these threats.

Where is it happening?

The current warming trend is undeniably the result of human activity since the mid-twentieth century, and it is happening at an unparalleled rate spanning millennia. Human activity has indisputably warmed the atmosphere, oceans, and land, as well as causing vast and rapid changes in the atmosphere, ocean, cryosphere, and biosphere. Satellites and other technology developments have allowed scientists to glimpse the overall picture of global climate change. Carbon dioxide and other gases were shown to trap heat in the mid-nineteenth century. Many NASA equipment fly because of their potential to influence the transmission of infrared energy through the atmosphere. There is no doubt that higher quantities of greenhouse gases will cause the Earth to warm.

When Will Climate Change Be Unavoidable?

Humans have already caused considerable climate change, and we have set additional changes in motion. If we stopped producing greenhouse gases today, global temperatures would begin to level off within a few years. Temperatures would thereafter plateau but remain much above sea level for millennia. The time lag between what we do and when we experience it is less than a decade. While the effects of human actions on the Earth's climate to date are irreversible in the timeframe of humans alive now, every tiny amount of avoided future temperature rises results in less warming that would otherwise persist perpetually. According to the most recent predictions, global temperature would rise by 2.5 °C to 4.5 °C (4.5 °F to 8 °F) by 2100 unless significant action is taken to cut emissions. However, it may not be too late to avoid or mitigate some of the worst consequences of climate change. Climate change will necessitate a two-tiered response: "Mitigation" refers to limiting the flow of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. "Adaptation" refers to learning to live with and adapt to climate change that has already begun. The crucial question is how much carbon dioxide and other pollutants we will emit in the coming years.
